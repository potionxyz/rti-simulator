about this project

radio tomographic imaging (RTI) reconstructs a 3D picture of a room from how wireless signals attenuate as they pass through it. eight virtual ESP32 nodes are placed at the corners of a 2m cube. the 28 unique links between them form the "radio rays" — analogous to X-rays in a medical CT scan.

when a human body enters the room, it absorbs RF energy and causes a measurable drop in signal strength on any link whose line of sight it intersects. the forward model simulates this using Siddon's ray-tracing algorithm from medical imaging — calculating exact path lengths through 1,000 voxels without iterative ray-marching.

the hard part is the inverse problem: given 28 noisy measurements, recover the 1,000-voxel attenuation image. the system is severely underdetermined, so the pseudo-inverse is numerically unstable. i solve it using Tikhonov regularisation with LDLT decomposition — stabilising the matrix by penalising extreme voxel values.

best-case localisation error is 0.025m (back-projection, noiseless) and 0.061m under Tikhonov with λ = 1.0. the solver stays usable up to roughly 1.5 dB of Gaussian RSSI noise — the realistic operating region for cheap ESP32 hardware. all the heavy linear algebra runs in under 50ms per scene on a laptop; the 3D viewer is a pure Three.js export with no backend.
